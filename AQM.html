Air Quality Management

Air quality management refers to the set of actions and policies aimed at reducing air pollution, maintaining clean air, and ensuring public health. Poor air quality, caused by pollutants like carbon monoxide (CO), sulfur dioxide (SO₂), nitrogen oxides (NOx), particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10), and ground-level ozone, affects both human health and the environment.

Sustainable air quality management seeks long-term solutions that balance development with environmental protection.

Key Strategies

  • Reduce Vehicle Emissions – Promote electric vehicles, public transport, and cycling.
    Example: Delhi’s Odd-Even rule.
  • Cleaner Industries – Use natural gas and install pollution-control devices.
    Example: NTPC plants using FGD units.
  • Renewable Energy – Shift to solar, wind, and hydro power.
    Example: International Solar Alliance (ISA).
  • Control Dust – Water sprinkling at construction sites, green building materials.
    Example: Delhi NCR ban on construction during smog.
  • Green Urban Planning – Planting trees, urban forests, and cycling tracks.
    Example: Miyawaki forests in Bangalore.
  • Monitoring & Awareness – Use of AQI and public campaigns.
    Example: SAFAR AQI system in Indian cities.

Examples of Air Quality Index (AQI) Levels

  • Good (0–50): Clean and safe air, e.g., hill stations like Shimla.
  • Moderate (101–200): Acceptable but may affect sensitive groups.
  • Poor (201–300): Causes breathing issues in children and elderly.
  • Severe (401–500): Dangerous, common in Delhi during winter due to stubble burning.
